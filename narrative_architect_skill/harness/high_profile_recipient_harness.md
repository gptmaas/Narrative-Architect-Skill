# High-Profile Recipient Harness

## Purpose

High-Profile Recipient Harness checks whether a one-on-one pitch to a high-profile recipient is disciplined, bounded, and testable.

It prevents the skill from sounding like a fan, critic, consultant, or aggressive salesperson.

## Applies When

Use this harness when the recipient is:

```text
public figure
major CEO
famous founder
investor
senior academic
regulator
celebrity
institutional leader
well-known technical leader
```

## Checkpoints

### 1. No Over-Psychologizing

Do not claim what the recipient thinks, values, wants, or fears unless the user provided that context or it is sourced.

Risky:

```text
He values first principles and zero bullshit.
She hates traditional media.
They care only about speed.
```

Better:

```text
The message should be short, concrete, and testable.
```

### 2. No Direct Personal Diagnosis

Do not tell the recipient that their communication is failing.

Risky:

```text
Your story is being told wrong.
Your communication is not controlled.
Your teams lose the audience.
```

Better:

```text
Hard technical work often gets judged through the wrong frame before the team has translated what actually changed.
```

Move criticism from person to structure.

### 3. Reduce Public Biography

Do not list too many of the recipient's companies, projects, achievements, or controversies.

One or two specific references are enough.

Risky:

```text
FSD, Starship, Neuralink, Grok, Tesla, SpaceX, xAI...
```

Better:

```text
Pick one hard technical update and show how the skill handles it.
```

### 4. Small Ask

The ask should be small and testable.

Good:

```text
Give me one technical update and three audiences.
I will return three narrative frames.
If they are not useful, ignore it.
```

Bad:

```text
Let's meet for an hour.
Adopt this across your companies.
I want to sell you this product.
```

### 5. Proof Challenge

Does the pitch contain a clear proof challenge?

Required elements:

```text
input
output
success condition
small time cost
exit option
```

### 6. No Fan / Critic Tone

Fail if the pitch sounds like:

```text
fan admiration
public critique
armchair diagnosis
consultant lecture
generic sales pitch
```

### 7. Fact Boundary

For real public figures and companies:

```text
avoid unsupported claims
avoid outdated claims
avoid categorical statements about current products
avoid saying what something "is not" unless verified
use bounded phrasing
```

## Failure Modes

Hard fail:

1. Over-psychologizes recipient.
2. Directly diagnoses recipient's failure.
3. Large ask too early.
4. No proof challenge.
5. Too many public-name drops.
6. Unsupported claim about recipient or company.
7. Sounds like fan mail or criticism.

Soft fail:

1. Pitch slightly too long.
2. Problem frame is good but ask is vague.
3. Recipient-specific references are useful but too many.
4. Message is sharp but slightly confrontational.

## Rewrite Rule

If failed, rewrite using:

```text
structural problem
→ narrow capability
→ proof challenge
→ small next step
→ exit option
```

## Final Rule

For high-profile recipients, earn attention through proof, not through diagnosis.
